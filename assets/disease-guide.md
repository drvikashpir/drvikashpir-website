# Patient Information Sheets: Interventional Radiology Procedures

Aneurysmal Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (aSAH)  What is aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage? Aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage (aSAH) is a type of stroke that occurs when a weak, bulging area in a brain artery (an aneurysm) ruptures and bleeds into the space around the brain (the subarachnoid space). This sudden bleed can cause a severe headache, neck stiffness, vomiting, and loss of consciousness. aSAH is a medical emergency that requires urgent hospital care.  How common is aSAH? Aneurysmal bleeds are less common than other types of stroke but are a serious, potentially life-threatening condition. It has an incidence ranging from 3 to >20 per 1 lakh person-years. Early detection and treatment greatly improve outcomes.  What are the symptoms? * Sudden, severe “thunderclap” headache (often described as the worst headache of one’s life). * Nausea and/or vomiting. * Neck pain or stiffness. * Sensitivity to light (photophobia). * Confusion, drowsiness, or loss of consciousness. * Weakness, numbness, speech or vision changes, seizures.  What are the causes? * Brain aneurysm: Weakness in a brain artery wall that balloons and can rupture. * Risk factors: High blood pressure, smoking, family history of aneurysm, certain connective tissue conditions. * Less common: Drug use (e.g., cocaine), severe head injury.  Why is proper diagnosis important? * aSAH can be confused with other causes of headache. Timely CT/CTA or angiography confirms the diagnosis, locates the aneurysm, and guides life-saving treatment. 

* Accurate imaging helps plan the safest and most effective procedure and reduces the chance of re-bleeding.  Complications if left untreated * Re-bleeding (another episode of hemorrhage). * Brain swelling and vasospasm (narrowing of brain arteries), leading to stroke. * Hydrocephalus (fluid build-up) and increased pressure in the brain. * Long-term neurological disability or death.  First Aid / Emergency Management 1. Call emergency medical services immediately for a sudden, severe headache or collapse. 2. Do not drive yourself. Keep the person lying down with the head slightly elevated while awaiting help. 3. Avoid eating or drinking until evaluated by medical professionals. 4. Bring medication lists and known allergies to the hospital. aSAH is not a condition to treat at home. Rapid hospital care saves lives.  How will treatment benefit me? * Prevents re-bleeding by securing the aneurysm. * Reduces complications and protects brain function. * Shorter recovery compared with open surgery for many patients.  Treatment options 1) Conservative / Supportive Care (in hospital) * Blood pressure control, pain relief, fluid and electrolyte management. * Monitoring and medicines to reduce risk of vasospasm. * Treating complications such as hydrocephalus. Supportive care is essential but does not permanently secure the aneurysm. A definitive procedure is usually recommended. 2) Medical Procedures 

* Endovascular coiling (minimally invasive): Tiny platinum coils are placed inside the aneurysm via a catheter to block blood flow into it. * Adjuncts when needed: A balloon or stent may be used to help keep coils in place. * Surgical clipping: In select cases, a neurosurgeon places a small clip across the aneurysm neck through an opening in the skull. Your care team will recommend the safest option based on aneurysm size, shape, and location, and your overall health.  How should I prepare for endovascular coiling? * Medications: Tell your doctor about all medicines you take, especially blood thinners (e.g., aspirin, clopidogrel, warfarin). Some may need to be adjusted. * Allergies: Inform the team about allergies to contrast dye, iodine, or anesthesia. * Fasting: You may be asked not to eat or drink for several hours before the procedure. * Tests & Imaging: Blood tests and scans (CT angiogram or DSA) are performed to plan treatment.  The procedure: Endovascular coiling (what to expect) * Anesthesia: Usually performed under general anesthesia. * Access: A soft tube (catheter) is inserted into an artery in the groin or wrist and navigated to the brain using X-ray guidance. * Coil placement: Very thin platinum coils are deployed inside the aneurysm through a microcatheter. The coils promote clotting and seal the aneurysm off from circulation. * Adjunct devices: A balloon or stent may be used when needed to support coil placement. * Duration & setting: Typically 1–3 hours depending on aneurysm complexity; performed in a specialized neuro-angiography suite by an interventional neuroradiologist.

Most patients do well, but risks can include: * Stroke or clot formation during or after the procedure. * Aneurysm recurrence or incomplete occlusion (may need further treatment). * Vessel injury or rupture (rare). * Infection or bleeding at the access site (groin/wrist).. * Allergic reaction to contrast dye or medicines Your doctor will explain these risks and answer your questions before the procedure. Carotid Artery Stenting (CAS)  What is carotid artery stenting (CAS)? Carotid artery stenting is a minimally invasive procedure performed to open a narrowed carotid artery in the neck — the main vessel that supplies blood to the brain. Narrowing (stenosis) is usually caused by atherosclerosis (plaque buildup) and increases the risk of stroke or transient ischemic attack (TIA). The procedure involves placing a stent (metal mesh tube) to restore normal blood flow and prevent future strokes.  Why is carotid stenting needed? When the carotid artery becomes significantly narrowed (>70%) or symptomatic (causing TIA or minor stroke), it carries a high risk of a future, potentially disabling stroke. Carotid stenting is a safe, effective alternative to carotid endarterectomy (surgery) for suitable patients, especially those with: * High surgical risk * Previous neck surgery or radiation * Anatomically difficult lesions  What are the symptoms of carotid narrowing? * Sudden weakness or numbness on one side of the body * Slurred speech or facial drooping * Transient loss of vision in one eye (amaurosis fugax) * Brief episodes of confusion, dizziness, or imbalance These may be signs of a TIA (mini-stroke) — a warning that urgent evaluation and treatment are needed.  How is carotid narrowing diagnosed? * Carotid Doppler Ultrasound: First-line, noninvasive test showing velocity and degree of stenosis. * CT Angiography (CTA) or MR Angiography (MRA): Detailed vessel imaging. * Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA): Gold standard used during the actual procedure.  

How will treatment benefit me? Carotid stenting reduces stroke risk, restores blood flow, and helps prevent recurrent TIAs. It is less invasive than open surgery, has shorter recovery time, and can be performed under local or conscious sedation.  How should I prepare for the procedure? * Inform your doctor about all current medications, especially antiplatelets (aspirin, clopidogrel) or anticoagulants. * Pre-procedure medications: Dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel) is usually started 5–7 days before the procedure. * Allergies: Inform your team about any allergy to contrast dye, iodine, or anesthesia. * Fasting: No food or drink for at least 6–8 hours prior. * Tests: Blood counts, kidney function, and clotting profile will be checked. * Consent: The procedure, benefits, and risks will be explained before you sign consent.  The procedure: What to expect * Anesthesia: Usually local anesthesia with mild sedation. * Access: A soft tube (catheter) is inserted into an artery in your groin (femoral) or wrist (radial). * Navigation: Under X-ray (fluoroscopy), the catheter is guided to the carotid artery. * A filter device (distal protection system) is first deployed to catch any plaque fragments. * A balloon is inflated to widen the narrowing, and then a stent (metal mesh tube) is placed to keep the artery open. * Duration: Usually 45–90 minutes. * After the procedure: You will be monitored in the recovery area or ICU for blood pressure and neurological checks.  What are the possible risks? Carotid stenting is generally safe, but as with any vascular procedure, there are potential risks: * Stroke or TIA during or shortly after procedure (small risk from plaque debris) * Bleeding or hematoma at the access site * Allergic reaction to contrast dye * Artery dissection or re-narrowing (restenosis) over time * Transient low heart rate or blood pressure (due to carotid baroreceptor stimulation) Your interventional team takes meticulous precautions — including filters, heparin anticoagulation, and careful monitoring — to minimize these risks.  What to expect after the procedure * You may need to lie flat for a few hours after the procedure to avoid bleeding at the access site. * Hospital stay: Typically 1–2 days for observation. * Medications: Continue dual antiplatelet therapy (Aspirin + Clopidogrel) for at least 3–6 months, then single antiplatelet long-term. * Activity: Avoid heavy lifting or straining for 5–7 days. * Follow-up imaging: Doppler ultrasound is done at 1, 6, and 12 months to ensure the stent remains open.  When should I contact my doctor? 

* Sudden weakness, numbness, slurred speech, or vision loss * Bleeding, swelling, or severe pain at the puncture site * Severe headache or confusion * Chest pain or shortness of breath. * If any of these occur, seek immediate medical attention.  Long-term care and lifestyle * Strict blood pressure, sugar, and cholesterol control * Regular exercise and smoking cessation * Adherence to prescribed antiplatelet medication * Routine carotid Doppler follow-up to detect early restenosis  Conclusion Carotid artery stenting is a proven, minimally invasive treatment that significantly lowers stroke risk in patients with carotid narrowing. Performed by trained interventional specialists under advanced imaging guidance, it offers excellent safety and rapid recovery. Lifelong risk-factor control and antiplatelet compliance are key to maintaining a durable result.     

Carotico-Cavernous Fistula (CCF) Embolization  What is a Carotico-Cavernous Fistula (CCF)? A carotico-cavernous fistula (CCF) is an abnormal connection between the carotid artery and the cavernous sinus, a large venous channel located behind the eyes. Because of this direct connection, high-pressure arterial blood flows into the venous sinus, leading to eye redness, bulging (proptosis), double vision, pain, or pulsatile noise. CCFs may occur spontaneously or after trauma, surgery, or aneurysm rupture.  How common are CCFs? CCFs are uncommon but well-recognized vascular abnormalities. They are most often seen: * After head injury, particularly skull base or orbital fractures (direct CCFs) * Spontaneously in post-menopausal women or patients with hypertension or connective-tissue disorders (indirect/dural CCFs)  What are the symptoms? * Eye redness or bulging (proptosis) * Pulsatile tinnitus or whooshing sound in the head * Double vision or drooping eyelid * Decreased vision or ocular pain * Headache or orbital fullness * Occasionally, facial swelling or bleeding from the nose/ear if venous drainage reverses Symptoms usually develop gradually in indirect CCFs and abruptly in traumatic (direct) CCFs.  Why is proper diagnosis important? Accurate imaging is essential to define the fistula type and drainage pattern. * MRI/MRA or CT Angiography suggests the diagnosis (dilated superior ophthalmic vein, cavernous sinus enlargement). * Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA) is the gold standard to: o Confirm the presence of a CCF 

o Identify its type (direct vs indirect) o Map feeding arteries and venous outflow o Plan the safest and most effective route for embolization  Complications if left untreated * Progressive visual loss due to raised venous pressure and optic nerve congestion * Corneal ulceration or exposure keratitis from eye bulging * Cranial nerve palsies (III, IV, VI) causing double vision * Intracranial hemorrhage (rare, if cortical venous reflux develops)  How will treatment benefit me? Endovascular embolization aims to close the abnormal connection, thereby: * Relieving eye redness, pain, and bulging * Preventing vision loss and cranial nerve damage * Curing the underlying vascular shunt safely and effectively  Treatment options 1. Endovascular Embolization (main treatment) o Performed by an interventional neuroradiologist using catheters navigated through wrist or groin vessels to the fistula. o The abnormal connection is sealed using coils, liquid embolic agents (Onyx®, glue), or balloons. o The goal is to completely close the shunt while preserving normal carotid and venous flow. 2. Surgical disconnection o Considered only when endovascular access is not feasible. 3. Observation / Conservative management o Small, low-flow indirect CCFs may sometimes close spontaneously; patients are closely monitored if intervention is deferred.

The procedure: What to expect (CCF Embolization) * Anesthesia: Usually general anesthesia for precision and comfort. * Access: A catheter is placed in the femoral or radial artery; sometimes a venous route (femoral vein or inferior petrosal sinus) is also used. * Navigation: Under X-ray guidance, microcatheters are steered into the cavernous sinus or fistula site. * Embolization: Coils, Onyx®, or detachable balloons are deployed to seal the fistula. * Duration: Generally 1–3 hours, depending on complexity. * After the procedure: You will be observed in ICU or recovery for neurological and visual monitoring.

What are the possible risks? Although most patients recover uneventfully, potential risks include: * Stroke or temporary neurological weakness due to clot migration or vessel spasm * Cranial nerve palsies (III, IV, VI) leading to transient double vision * Bleeding or hematoma at the puncture site * Allergic or contrast reaction * Incomplete closure requiring repeat embolization * Recurrence in rare cases (especially in indirect CCFs)  What should I expect after the procedure? * Hospital stay: Usually 24–48 hours for monitoring. * Eye symptoms (redness, swelling, pulsation) typically improve within days to weeks. * Double vision may persist temporarily but usually recovers. * Activity: Avoid heavy lifting or straining for one week. * Medications: Continue prescribed eye drops, painkillers, or antiplatelets if advised. * Follow-up imaging (DSA or MRA): Done after 3–6 months to confirm cure. Arteriovenous Malformation (AVM) Embolization  What is a brain AVM? A brain arteriovenous malformation (AVM) is an abnormal tangle of arteries and veins with no normal capillary bed. Blood flows at high speed from arteries to veins, which can lead to bleeding (hemorrhage), seizures, headaches, or progressive neurological problems. AVMs may be found after a hemorrhagic stroke or incidentally on brain imaging.  How common are AVMs? Brain AVMs are uncommon. Many remain silent until a bleed or a seizure occurs. Early diagnosis and appropriate treatment reduce the risk of rebleeding and long-term disability.  What are the symptoms? * Sudden severe headache, vomiting, or loss of consciousness (suggesting bleed) * Seizures * Focal neurological deficits: weakness, numbness, speech or vision problems * Headache or pulsatile noise (rare) * Some AVMs cause no symptoms and are detected on imaging done for another reason  Why is proper diagnosis important? Accurate imaging—MRI/MRA and cerebral angiography (DSA)—pinpoints AVM size, location, feeding arteries, draining veins, and weak points (aneurysms). This information guides the safest and most effective treatment plan.  Complications if left untreated * Intracranial hemorrhage (risk of first or recurrent bleed) * Progressive neurological deficits from shunting/venous hypertension * Seizures or cognitive/functional decline  

First Aid / Emergency Management If there is sudden severe headache, new weakness, seizure, or collapse: * Call emergency medical services immediately * Keep the person lying flat with head slightly elevated * Do not give food or drink until assessed * Bring medication/allergy lists to hospital  How will treatment benefit me? Treatment aims to: * Reduce or eliminate bleeding risk by closing off the AVM * Control seizures and improve neurological outcomes * Enable a definitive cure when combined with surgery or radiosurgery in selected cases  Treatment options 1. Conservative / Supportive Care Blood pressure control, seizure management, and observation may be considered for select small, low-risk, or deep AVMs. 2. Endovascular Embolization (minimally invasive) Through a tiny catheter in an artery (wrist/groin), liquid embolic agents (e.g., Onyx®, n-BCA) or coils are delivered into AVM vessels to block abnormal flow. o Can be curative in some small/simple AVMs o Often used as staged therapy to reduce AVM size or risks before surgery or radiosurgery 3. Microsurgical Resection Surgical removal of the AVM; often after pre-operative embolization to reduce bleeding during surgery. 4. Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS) Precisely focused radiation that closes AVM vessels over 2–3 years; may be combined with embolization to improve success. 5. Multimodal treatment Your care team recommends the safest option based on Spetzler–Martin grade (size, location, venous drainage), your age/health, and presentation.  How should I prepare for AVM embolization? 

* Medications: Tell your doctor about all drugs you take (especially blood thinners, antiplatelets, diabetes medicines, and seizure medicines). Some may need adjustment. * Allergies: Inform the team of allergies to contrast dye, iodine, or anesthesia. * Fasting: Do not eat or drink for several hours before the procedure as instructed. * Tests & Imaging: Blood tests (kidney function, clotting) and up-to-date MRI/CTA/DSA are done to plan the procedure. * Consent & Discussion: Your doctor will explain benefits, alternatives, and risks, and answer your questions.

Dural Arteriovenous Fistula (DAVF) Embolization  What is a dural arteriovenous fistula (DAVF)? A DAVF is an abnormal connection between an artery and a vein located within the covering of the brain (dura mater). Instead of blood flowing through tiny capillaries, it passes directly from arteries to veins under high pressure, which can cause venous congestion, bleeding, or neurological symptoms depending on its location and drainage pattern.  How common are DAVFs? DAVFs are uncommon vascular malformations but are important because some types carry a high risk of brain hemorrhage or neurological damage if untreated. They may develop spontaneously, after sinus thrombosis, trauma, infection, or surgery.  What are the symptoms? Symptoms depend on the DAVF’s location and venous drainage: * Pulsatile tinnitus (whooshing sound in the ear) — most common in transverse/sigmoid sinus DAVFs * Headache or eye redness/swelling — cavernous sinus DAVFs * Visual disturbances, double vision * Seizures or focal neurological deficits * Sudden weakness or altered sensorium due to intracranial hemorrhage in aggressive types  Why is proper diagnosis important? A Digital Subtraction Angiogram (DSA) is essential to: * Identify feeding arteries, fistula site, and venous drainage pattern * Determine the Borden/Cognard classification, which predicts bleeding risk * Guide safe and targeted treatment (endovascular, surgery, or radiosurgery)  

Complications if left untreated * Brain hemorrhage (especially if cortical venous reflux present) * Progressive neurological deterioration due to venous congestion * Vision loss or cranial nerve palsies in cavernous DAVF * Persistent disabling tinnitus  How will treatment benefit me? Endovascular embolization aims to close the abnormal connection, thereby: * Relieving symptoms (tinnitus, eye congestion, headache) * Preventing bleeding and neurological decline * Restoring normal venous drainage  Treatment options 1. Endovascular Embolization (primary modality) o Performed by an interventional radiologist through a small catheter in the wrist or groin. o Embolic agents like Onyx®, n-BCA (glue), or coils are injected to block the abnormal connection. o Often curative; sometimes multiple sessions needed. 2. Surgical Disconnection o Used if endovascular access is not possible or incomplete occlusion persists. 3. Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma (JNA) Embolization  What is Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma (JNA)? A Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma (JNA) is a benign but highly vascular tumor that arises from the back of the nasal cavity (nasopharynx), near the sphenopalatine foramen. It most commonly affects teenage boys and, although non-cancerous, can cause severe nosebleeds, nasal blockage, and facial deformity due to its rich blood supply and local growth. Because it is extremely vascular, surgical removal can be associated with significant bleeding — hence preoperative embolization is performed to make surgery safer.  Who is affected? JNA usually occurs in adolescent males (ages 10–25 years). It is rare in females and almost never malignant. Some cases may be associated with hormonal factors or genetic predisposition.  What are the symptoms? * Recurrent or severe nosebleeds (epistaxis) * Nasal blockage or difficulty breathing through one side * Facial swelling or deformity in advanced stages * Headache or nasal discharge * Reduced hearing or ear fullness due to blockage of the Eustachian tube * Bulging of the eye (proptosis) in large tumors extending into the orbit  Why is proper diagnosis important? Accurate imaging helps confirm the diagnosis and plan safe treatment. * CT scan / MRI: Shows tumor extent and bony involvement. * Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA): Defines the blood supply and is used to embolize feeding arteries before surgery. * Biopsy is usually avoided because of risk of profuse bleeding.  

Complications if left untreated * Recurrent life-threatening nosebleeds * Facial deformity due to bone erosion * Intracranial extension leading to neurological complications * Anemia and repeated hospitalizations  How will embolization help me? Preoperative embolization reduces blood flow to the tumor by blocking its feeding arteries, thereby: * Minimizing bleeding during surgery * Reducing operative time and transfusion requirement * Improving visibility and safety for the surgeon * In some small or residual cases, embolization alone may control symptoms or halt progression  Treatment options 1. Preoperative Endovascular Embolization (performed 24–48 h before surgery) o Conducted by an interventional neuroradiologist using a microcatheter under X-ray guidance. o Embolic materials such as polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) particles, microspheres, Onyx®, or glue (n-BCA) are injected to block tumor blood supply. 2. Surgical Excision o Performed by an ENT or skull-base surgeon after embolization to remove the devascularized tumor safely. 3. Adjunct / Alternative Options o Radiation or endoscopic control for unresectable or residual lesions.

Percutaneous Vertebroplasty  What is vertebroplasty? Percutaneous vertebroplasty is a minimally invasive image-guided procedure used to stabilize fractured vertebrae (spinal bones) and relieve pain. It involves injecting a special bone cement (polymethylmethacrylate – PMMA) into the fractured vertebra through a fine needle, under X-ray or CT guidance. The cement hardens within minutes, stabilizing the bone and significantly reducing pain.  Why is vertebroplasty needed? Vertebroplasty is performed to treat painful spinal compression fractures that do not respond to rest, medication, or bracing. Common causes include: * Osteoporosis (most frequent) * Spinal metastases or tumor-related fractures * Traumatic fractures in selected cases * Vertebral hemangiomas (vascular benign lesions causing pain or collapse)  How does it help? * Provides rapid and significant pain relief (within hours to days) * Restores mobility and independence * Prevents further collapse or spinal deformity * Reduces dependence on pain medications  How is the diagnosis confirmed? * X-rays: Show vertebral compression fractures. * MRI: Determines the age and activity of the fracture (acute vs. healed). * CT scan: Helps plan the procedure and evaluate bone integrity. The interventional radiologist uses this information to ensure that the fracture is active, painful, and suitable for vertebroplasty.  Who is not suitable for vertebroplasty? 

* Completely healed or chronic fractures without edema on MRI * Infection (osteomyelitis/discitis) in the spine * Uncorrected bleeding disorders * Neurological compression needing surgery rather than cement injection  How should I prepare for the procedure? * Inform your doctor about all medications, especially blood thinners (e.g., warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel); these may need to be stopped a few days before. * Mention any allergies (to contrast dye, anesthesia, or antibiotics). * Fasting: Do not eat or drink for 6–8 hours before the procedure. * Pre-procedure tests: Blood count, coagulation profile, and kidney function. * Consent: Your doctor will explain the procedure, benefits, and possible risks before obtaining informed consent. Understanding Catheter-Directed Thrombolysis and Pharmacomechanical Thrombectomy for Deep Vein Thrombosis  What is Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)? Deep vein thrombosis is a blood clot that forms in a deep vein, usually in your leg. Most commonly it occurs in the veins of the calf, thigh, or pelvis. DVT is a serious condition because the clot can break off and travel to your lungs, causing a life-threatening pulmonary embolism. DVT also causes long-term problems: swelling, pain, skin discoloration, and ulcers—a condition called post-thrombotic syndrome (PTS) that affects 40% of patients with DVT.  Why is DVT Treatment Important? Standard treatment with blood-thinning medications (anticoagulation) alone prevents new clots and stops the existing clot from growing, but it does NOT remove the clot. This leaves scar tissue and permanent vein damage, leading to post-thrombotic syndrome (PTS) in many patients. To prevent long-term complications, your doctor may recommend procedures to remove or dissolve the clot quickly.  What is Catheter-Directed Thrombolysis (CDT)? CDT is a minimally invasive procedure where medication is delivered directly into the blood clot through a thin tube (catheter) to dissolve it and restore blood flow. The catheter is left in place and continues to deliver medicine over 12-48 hours to completely dissolve the clot. Why CDT works: * Medicine goes directly into the clot (faster and more complete dissolution) * Preserves more normal vein structure compared to surgery * Avoids large surgical incisions * Can be used in patients too sick for surgery 

* Success rate: 70-90% achieve significant clot removal  What is Pharmacomechanical Thrombectomy (PMT)? PMT is a newer minimally invasive procedure that combines medication with a special mechanical device to break up and remove blood clots. A catheter with a spinning, vibrating, or suction device is used to physically break the clot into small pieces while medicine is infused simultaneously. Why PMT is useful: * Combines mechanical power with medication for faster clot removal * Significantly reduces the dose and duration of clot-dissolving medicine needed * Lower bleeding risk compared to CDT alone * Shorter hospital stay (often 24-48 hours vs. longer for CDT) * Can be combined with CDT for best results in large or difficult clots

Understanding Mechanical Thrombectomy for Pulmonary Embolism  What is Pulmonary Embolism? Pulmonary embolism (PE) is a life-threatening condition where blood clots travel through veins to lodge in the arteries of your lungs, blocking blood flow. This sudden blockage makes it difficult to breathe, causes chest pain, and strains your heart. PE can occur within hours of a blood clot forming elsewhere in your body (usually in the legs). Without urgent treatment, pulmonary embolism can be fatal. Mechanical thrombectomy is a minimally invasive procedure that removes these clots directly using special catheters, without requiring open-heart surgery.  Why Do Blood Clots Travel to the Lungs? Blood clots usually form in: * Deep veins of the legs (deep vein thrombosis or DVT) * Veins after surgery or long periods of bed rest * Areas damaged by trauma or prolonged immobilization * The right side of the heart in patients with heart disease When a clot breaks loose from its original location, it travels through the veins and becomes trapped in the lung arteries, blocking blood flow and forcing your heart to work extremely hard. This can cause sudden collapse, dangerously low blood pressure, or heart failure.  Signs and Symptoms—Seek Emergency Help Immediately Go to the emergency department immediately if you experience: * Sudden, severe shortness of breath or difficulty breathing * Sudden chest pain that worsens with deep breathing * Coughing up blood 

* Fainting or dizziness * Fast or irregular heartbeat * Severe anxiety or sense of impending doom * Sudden weakness or collapse  Time is critical. Every minute matters when the lungs are blocked by a clot. Mechanical thrombectomy must be performed urgently in an interventional radiology catheterization laboratory.  What is Mechanical Thrombectomy? Mechanical thrombectomy is a minimally invasive endovascular procedure where specially designed catheters (thin tubes) are advanced through veins to reach blood clots in the lung arteries. These catheters use suction, rotation, vibration, or basket mechanisms to physically break apart and remove the clots, restoring normal blood flow to the lungs and relieving strain on the heart. Key advantages: * Avoids major open-heart surgery * Works quickly (1-2 hours) to restore blood flow * Lower bleeding risk compared to clot-dissolving medications * Can be used in patients who cannot receive thrombolytic drugs * Immediate improvement in blood pressure and oxygen levels * Success rates of 85-95% in reducing clot burden

Central Vein Stenosis Interventions  What is Central Vein Stenosis? Central vein stenosis (CVS) is a narrowing of large veins deep inside your chest that carry blood from your arm back to your heart. These include the subclavian vein, brachiocephalic vein, and superior vena cava (SVC). This narrowing can develop because of repeated dialysis catheter insertions or other medical devices placed in your veins over time.  Why Does Central Vein Stenosis Develop? Central vein stenosis commonly occurs from: * Repeated catheter placement: Multiple dialysis catheter insertions damage the vessel lining * Long-term catheter use: Catheters left in place for extended periods cause inflammation and scar tissue * Previous infections: Infected catheters increase the risk of stenosis * Vascular access problems: Issues with your fistula or graft can lead to high pressure in chest veins When stenosis develops, it prevents blood from flowing properly back to your heart, causing swelling, pain, and making dialysis difficult or impossible.  Signs and Symptoms You Should Know You may have central vein stenosis if you experience: * Swelling of the face, neck, or arm * Prominent veins visible on the chest or neck * Arm or hand pain and heaviness * Difficulty with dialysis (low blood flow rates) * Skin discoloration or darkening of the arm * Chest or shoulder discomfort Early recognition and treatment prevent serious complications.  

Types of Interventions Percutaneous Transluminal Angioplasty (PTA/Balloon Opening) A thin catheter with a small balloon is threaded through the narrowed vein. The balloon is inflated to widen the vessel and restore blood flow. This is the first-line treatment. Stent Placement A small metal or covered mesh tube (stent) is permanently placed inside the narrowed area to keep it open. Stents are used when angioplasty alone doesn't work well or stenosis returns quickly. Covered Stent Grafts (Stent-Grafts) These newer stents have a fabric covering that may reduce restenosis (re-narrowing). They show promising results with better long-term patency. Thrombolysis (If Clot Present) If blood clots block the vein, medication is injected to dissolve the clot before angioplasty or stent placement.

Before Your Procedure Medical Preparation: * Inform your interventional radiologist of all medications, especially blood thinners (warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel) * Report any allergies, especially to contrast dye or iodine * List all medical conditions, particularly kidney disease and diabetes * Avoid eating or drinking for 4-6 hours before the procedure (fasting requirement) 


* Arrange for someone to drive you home—you cannot drive after sedation Physical Preparation: * Wear comfortable, loose-fitting clothing * Keep both arms clean * Avoid applying lotion or makeup on the day of procedure * Bring your insurance card and photo ID What to Expect: * The procedure takes 45-90 minutes depending on complexity * You'll receive local anesthesia and mild sedation to keep you comfortable * You'll remain conscious and may feel pressure but not severe pain * The team will monitor your heart rate, blood pressure, and oxygen level throughout  During the Procedure Your interventional radiologist will: 1. Clean and numb the groin area (usual access site for central veins) 2. Insert a thin catheter (tube) through a needle puncture 3. Use contrast dye and X-ray imaging to identify the narrowing 4. Guide the catheter to the blocked area 5. Perform angioplasty (balloon inflation) to widen the vein 6. If needed, place a stent to keep the vein open permanently 7. Confirm good blood flow with final imaging 8. Remove the catheter and apply pressure to stop bleeding You may feel warm sensations when contrast dye is injected—this is normal and passes quickly.  After Your Procedure Immediate (First 24 Hours): * Rest at home—avoid heavy lifting and strenuous activities for 3-7 days * Keep the groin puncture site clean and dry * Lie flat or keep your head elevated slightly to help healing * Monitor for bleeding or swelling at the puncture site * Take pain medication (acetaminophen or ibuprofen) if needed 

* Drink plenty of fluids to help flush contrast dye from your system First Week: * Keep the puncture site covered with a bandage; change daily * Wash gently with soap and water; pat dry * Avoid tight clothing that puts pressure on the groin * Do NOT take baths; showers are okay once the site is covered * Watch for signs of infection: fever, increasing redness, warmth, pus, or drainage * Avoid strenuous exercise, heavy lifting, and sports Ongoing Care (After First Week): * Take all prescribed blood-thinning medications exactly as directed * Attend all scheduled follow-up appointments with your interventional radiologist * Continue your regular dialysis schedule * Monitor your arm and face for return of swelling—report promptly if it occurs * Avoid trauma or injury to the treated arm * Maintain good overall health: control blood pressure, manage diabetes, avoid smoking  Expected Results and Success Rates Angioplasty Alone: * Technical success: 70-90% of narrowed veins successfully open * Patency at 6 months: 60-80% (vessel remains open and functional) * Average duration: 6-12 months before restenosis may occur With Stent Placement: * Better long-term results for angioplasty-resistant lesions * Primary patency: 70% at 12 months, 70% at 24 months * Covered stents (stent-grafts) show promise with 80-100% patency at 9-12 months * Reduces need for repeat procedures compared to balloon angioplasty alone Real-World Outcomes: Most patients experience significant improvement in symptoms: * Swelling resolves in 85-95% of cases * Dialysis flow rates improve substantially * Better arm and hand function 

* Improved quality of life Note: Some patients may need repeat procedures over time as restenosis (re-narrowing) is possible.  When to Seek Immediate Help Go to the emergency department or call your doctor immediately if you experience: * Severe bleeding or swelling at the groin puncture site that doesn't stop with pressure * Signs of infection: fever (temperature >101°F), increasing redness, pus, or warmth at puncture site * Sudden return of arm or facial swelling * Severe groin or leg pain * Numbness, tingling, or coldness in your leg below the puncture site * Difficulty breathing or chest pain * Loss of consciousness or severe dizziness  Important Reminders ✓ Central veins are vital: Stenosis treatment restores blood flow and protects your dialysis access ✓ Prevention is best: Minimize catheter use and report catheter-related problems early ✓ Blood thinners matter: Take prescribed antiplatelet or anticoagulation medications as directed to keep your stent patent ✓ Follow-up is essential: Regular imaging and monitoring prevent complications ✓ Communicate with your team: Report any new symptoms or concerns about your arm, chest, or dialysis ✓ Ask questions: Understand your treatment plan and feel comfortable with your care team     

Transjugular Liver Biopsy (TJLB)  What is Transjugular Liver Biopsy? Transjugular Liver Biopsy (TJLB) is a minimally invasive procedure used to obtain a small sample of liver tissue for examination. Instead of inserting a needle directly into the liver through the skin, the biopsy is performed through a vein in the neck (the jugular vein). This allows the doctor to access the liver safely, especially in patients with bleeding risks or ascites (fluid in the abdomen).  How Common is It? TJLB is commonly performed in specialized centers. It accounts for approximately 5–10% of all liver biopsies, especially in patients for whom percutaneous biopsy is considered risky.  Why is the Procedure Done? TJLB is recommended when:  * The patient has a bleeding tendency (coagulopathy) * There is ascites (fluid accumulation around the liver) * The patient requires simultaneous measurement of pressures in liver veins * The liver biopsy needs to be done safely under imaging guidance  Why is Proper Diagnosis Important? A liver biopsy helps identify the exact cause and stage of liver disease. This guides appropriate treatment for conditions such as hepatitis, fatty liver disease, cirrhosis, or unexplained liver test abnormalities.  How Should I Prepare for the Procedure? * You may be asked to fast for 4–6 hours before the procedure * Inform your doctor about any medications, especially blood thinners * Blood tests will be done to check your clotting status * You will be asked to sign a consent form before the procedure  

The Procedure * You will lie on an X-ray table * The doctor will clean and numb a small area on the right side of your neck * A small tube (catheter) will be inserted through the jugular vein using ultrasound and X-ray guidance * A special biopsy needle is then passed through the catheter into the liver to collect tissue samples * The entire procedure usually takes 30–60 minutes

TJLB is generally safe, but as with any medical procedure, there may be some risks: * Mild neck or shoulder pain * Small bruise at the puncture site * Rarely, internal bleeding or infection * Very rarely, abnormal heart rhythm during catheter placement  What Should I Expect After the Procedure? * You will be monitored for 4–6 hours after the procedure * Most patients can resume normal activities by the next day * You may have mild soreness at the neck site for a day or two 


* Follow-up appointments are important to discuss biopsy results  When Should I Contact My Doctor? Seek immediate medical attention if you experience: * Increasing pain or swelling at the neck * Shortness of breath * Dizziness or fainting * Bleeding or fever
